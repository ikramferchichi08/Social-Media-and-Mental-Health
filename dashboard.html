PC1 — General Psychological Burden
38.4% of variance · Eigenvalue 4.99
All 13 variables load positively. Highest: concentration (0.78), easily_distracted (0.76), distracted (0.72), depression_freq (0.71). Represents overall social-media-related mental health strain. This axis is the primary driver of the clustering segmentation.
PC2 — Social Comparison vs. Passive Usage
9.3% of variance · Eigenvalue 1.21
compare_self (+0.54) and seek_validation (+0.47) load positively; daily_usage (−0.38) and no_purpose (−0.45) load negatively. Distinguishes active social comparers from passive heavy users.
PC3 — Emotional Response to Comparisons
8.6% of variance · Eigenvalue 1.11
comparison_feeling (0.78) and seek_validation (0.53) dominate. Captures how individuals feel about the comparisons they make on social media.
PC4 — Sleep Disruption / PC5 — Usage Intensity
7.4% + 6.3% · Combined 13.7%
PC4: sleep_issues (0.65) as dominant contributor — secondary dimension partially independent of PC1. PC5: high usage hours without general distractibility (daily_usage +0.49, easily_distracted −0.41).

06 · Conclusions

Key Findings & Implications

Three statistically robust, interpretable profiles emerged. Age — not gender — is the key demographic differentiator.

🔴 43.2% at high risk
The largest cluster — young adults (<24 yrs) spending 4–5h/day on social media with high depression, poor concentration, and sleep issues. The primary target for digital wellness interventions.
🔵 41.4% moderate risk
Moderate usage (~2–3h/day) with mid-range mental health scores. Not acutely at risk but potentially vulnerable to escalating into Cluster 1 patterns.
🟢 15.4% resilient baseline
Older users (avg 33.7 yrs), limited usage (~1–2h/day), minimal impact across all dimensions. Serves as a healthy reference population for future studies.
Age as a protective factor
A 10-year difference in average age between Cluster 1 (23.5 yrs) and Cluster 3 (33.7 yrs) suggests life experience or different usage patterns buffer against negative effects.
Gender is not a driver
Contrary to some prior literature, χ²=18.46 (p=0.298) shows no significant gender stratification in cluster membership.
PC1 drives everything
38.4% of all variance is a single "general psychological burden" factor — and it is the axis along which the three clusters primarily separate.
Limitations
Cross-sectional design — causality cannot be established. Sample biased toward young adults. Self-report bias. Silhouette of 0.224 indicates soft cluster boundaries.
